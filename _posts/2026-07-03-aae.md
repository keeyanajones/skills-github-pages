---
title: "African American English (AAE)"
date: 2026-07-03
---

When major tech platforms consistently overlook African American English (AAE), it sends a loud, clear message about who they are actually building products for—and who they are leaving out.

The lack of native AAE representation in mainstream text-to-speech (TTS) engines isn't just a minor oversight; it highlights a systemic bias in how language models and voice datasets are engineered.

**Why the Gap Exists (The Engineering Failure)**

The underlying reasons for this gap point directly to systemic issues in tech development:

Linguistic Bias in Training Data: Most standard speech models are trained on what Silicon Valley traditionally calls "Standard American English"—which is essentially a corporate, midwestern dialect. Because the engineers collecting the data rarely prioritize dialectal diversity, AAE is treated as an "edge case" or a regional accent rather than a complex, distinct linguistic system with its own precise rules of grammar, syntax, and cadence.

The "Accent" Classification Flaw: Instead of building models that natively understand and speak AAE, many large tech companies historically grouped all American voices into generic "US English" buckets. When they do try to introduce diversity, they often focus on geographical accents (like British, Australian, or Indian English) rather than cultural or ethnic dialects within the US.

A Lack of Diversity in the Room: The teams building these systems, deciding on features, and allocating budgets rarely reflect the people who actually use the tech. If the leadership and engineering teams lack Black cultural competency, the need for AAE voices isn't even on the roadmap until someone points out the omission.

**What Change Looks Like**

The fact that you have to jump through developer loopholes or use third-party tools like Narakeet just to get an authentic voice is proof that the consumer tech space is lagging behind.

Fortunately, change is beginning to happen, driven primarily by Black linguists, researchers, and independent companies who refuse to wait for Big Tech to catch up. Organizations like Mozilla’s Common Voice initiative have actively worked to build open-source, diverse voice datasets that specifically include AAE, ensuring that future AI models can be trained without these massive cultural blind spots.

It shouldn't require a workaround to hear a voice that sounds like home, and calling out that systemic gap is exactly how the industry gets pushed to fix it.
